About Me

I am an observational astronomer and instrument scientist interested in direct imaging of exoplanets/brown dwarfs, adaptive optics, and optical interferometry. I am currently a third year PhD candidate at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy (MPIA) in Heidelberg, Germany, working with Dr. Wolfgang Brandner and Dr. Laura Kreidberg in the Atmospheric Physics of Exoplanets (APEX) department. Modern astronomical instruments are highly complex systems that have to deal with all sorts of atmospheric effects and instrumental limitation. My goal is to combine our understanding of these complex systems with advanced numerical techniques to improve the quality and sensitivity of our observational data. Using these techniques I study the thermal emission of exoplanets and brown dwarfs trying to reveal their atmospheric properties. In particular, I am fascinated by the coldest known gas giants, the so called Y-dwarfs. These objects are the closest known analogues to Jupiter and still host many secrets!

Research

GRAVITY is a powerful, but highly complex instrument interfering the light of four telescopes thereby utilizing the 130 m baseline capabilities of the VLTI. Exactly these large baseline capabilities allow us to spatially resolve exoplanets very close to their hoststars and make direct observations possible. To make the observations possible GRAVITY comprises a wide range of sub-insruments compensating for e.g. atmospheric and pathlength errors. To quantitatively understand the stability of this complex machine I developed a simulation framework SIMTERFERE which makes use of the extensive ancillary data in GRAVITY observation data. The framework is capabale to reproduce major systematic efects in the measured coherent flux and allowed us to apply correction methods improving the stability down to telluric limitations.
The detection of exoplanets in the mid-infrared (> 3 µm), i.e. measuring the thermal emission of exoplanets, brings a big advantage due to less stringent contrast differences. However, when observing from ground, the high thermal background of the sky and telescope strongly limit the achievable sensitivity. Using Adaptive Optics (AO) systems to correct for atmospheric turbulence helps to concentrate the planet's flux into a smaller area on the detector, thus improving the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). On the other hand we found in this work that exactly this AO correction on the stellar and planetary light strongly correlates with temporal variations in the observed spatial background features. We conclude that these correlations are actually causal introduced by the deformable mirror in the AO system.
To improve the throughput and the Optical Path Differnce (OPD) compensation of the GRAVITY instrument at the VLTI we developed the Beam Compressor Differential Delay Line (BCDDL). The BCDDL is a compact and efficient design to compensate for differential OPDs between the fringe tracking and science target for the four telescopes of the VLTI while at the same time compressing the beam to fit into the GRAVITY instrument. I helped setting up a comprehensive testbed to develope the control software and then characterize and validate the performance of the BCDDL in the lab prior to its integration into GRAVITY. The BCDDL has been successfully integrated into GRAVITY and is now routinely used for observations, significantly enhancing the instrument's performance.
The squared visibility of an interferometric target is a measure of the spatial information of the object. Also intrumental effects such as the insopistonic angle error and vibrations directly affect the measured visibility. For observational preparation and the evaluation of the data, it is crucial to accurately predict the expected visibility loss based on the observing conditions and parameters. In this work, we developed an improved method to predict the visibility loss based on theoretical and emperical models.
